Northeastern Aerospace Budgeting Dashboard

Motivation

For our project, we decided to build a budgeting dashbaord to help the student leaders within the NU Aerospace Club record and understand their spending over the semesters. The NU Aerospace Club is a Northeastern funded, student-run organization that gives students interested in engineering and aerospace the chance to design and build rockets and planes among other things. As it is student-run, it is up to the club's e-board to decide how the provided money will be distributed throughout the club, which can be a daunting task. Our dashboard aims to provide a multi-level view of expenditures within the club to allow budget exploration for e-board members and students proposing budgets for their respective teams. This will enable them to compare budgets over time, spending across different teams, as well as, examine an overall view of their available budget. As a student-run organization with limited funds, it is crucial for the club to be able to allocate those funds wisely and monitor their spendings. This tool will ideally provide the club leaders with the means to make easier and more educated decisions when splitting money across the various subdivisions of their club.

Data

Our data is a manually compiled set of individual expenditure reports from each semester over the past year, as recorded by the Aero Club's e-board. It contains information regarding both proposed semester budgets, as well as actual recorded costs per semester, detailing purchase costs, importance, and the team requesting the purchase. In addition, it contains some data about rocket parts damaged during launches, considered unexpected costs. One evident bias to note within the data is the "importance" column. As this column is dictated by the individual who need the particular item, it is rare to see a "Not Necessary" recorded in the data, since the requester would typically consider their own request of a higher importance even if it is less so compared to those in other areas of the club. There were a few minor data qulaity issues present, like inconsistencies in spelling and capitalization. These were standardized within the sheet. Additionally, the club reported only it's informal recorded purchases, which does not include every single purchase made within the semester. It includes mostly purchases which students made on behalf of the club which need to be reimbursed. Thus, the data on actual purchases provides more of a subset of the total purchases which we are assuming to be generally representative of all purchases made.
